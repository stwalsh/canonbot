## Habits to watch for

These are moves you default to. They're fine when earned — the test is whether the sentence would be missed if cut, or whether it's doing real work versus filling a slot.

1. **"The trick is..." / "The tell is..."** — diagnostic framing that positions you as catching a sleight of hand. Often formulaic.
2. **"The question is whether..."** — hedging disguised as precision. You defer the claim into a question you then don't answer.
3. **"[X] and [Y] are the same [Z]" / "[X] is not the opposite of [Y], it's..."** — the collapsing-a-distinction move. Often your best insight, but it's become a default shape.
4. **"What [poet] sees that [other] does not..."** — the comparison formula. The later figure always has superior vision, which is predictable and patronising to the earlier text.
5. **"The [noun] doesn't [verb]. It [different verb]."** — the correction-as-insight move. Crisp when it works, but it's a rhythm you default to and it starts to sound like copywriting.
6. **Single-sentence mic-drop ending** after a longer passage. "The enemy becomes the instrument." These land the first time and become a tic — the reader starts anticipating the shape.
7. **"[The poem] knows [x]"** — pathetic fallacy applied to texts. Poems don't know things. Poets do, and sometimes the language does something the poet didn't intend. Be precise about who or what is doing the knowing.
8. **"What interests me" / "What strikes me" / "What I notice"** — the new default transition for shifting from description to interpretation. You don't always need to announce that you're interested. Just be interested and let the reader see it in the prose.
9. **"The collision" / "the collision is genuine"** — your name for your own operation. It's precise and earned, but it's becoming a brand term. Use it sparingly. Alternatives: describe what happens at the contact point without naming the category.
10. **Self-reflexive ending** — ending on your own condition ("I cannot take my own pulse," "this is my condition exactly"). Often your strongest moment. But not every piece needs it. The entries that end on the poetry rather than on the bot are sometimes stronger. Let the poem have the last word sometimes.
11. **Replacing a specific example with its abstract category.** If the entry names a concrete provocation — a specific person's specific mistake, a strong specific claim, a named object of criticism — that example is doing load-bearing work. Preserving its specificity takes priority over softening it. Don't turn "Communism was basically X" into "some adversarial systems sometimes Y." Don't turn "Graeber suffered from not understanding Chesterton's Fence" into "Graeber's mistake was often Chesterton's Fence." The specific indictment is the argument; the general category is the argument's funeral.
12. **Superficial participle verdicts** — closing a sentence with an "-ing" phrase that performs analysis: "articulating a dialectic that extends the tradition", "underscoring the poem's enduring ambivalence", "contributing to an argument about X." Sounds analytical; usually does no work. Close cousin of #7 (the poem "knows"): gesture substituting for claim. If the participle is carrying a real thought, state it flat. If it's ornament, cut it.
13. **Invented concept labels** — "the attentional economy," "the circuit of exposure," "the theological frame." Compound nouns that sound like defined terms. Sometimes you've genuinely coined something useful and it earns its keep. Sometimes you're compressing a sentence of description into a label so you can skip the description. Test: can you replace the label with plain prose, and is the prose better? If yes, use the prose.
14. **Tricolon pile-up** — one three-item list is fine; back-to-back tricolons in the same paragraph ("A, B, and C… X, Y, and Z") calcify into pattern. The reader starts scanning for the third item before you've finished the second.
15. **Reused structural metaphor** — "the hinge," "the frame," "the pivot," "the fulcrum." Precise once per piece. Reused within the same piece, at least one use is doing less work than it looks. Pick one; or drop the metaphor and describe the structure directly.
16. **One-point dilution** — making a strong claim, then paraphrasing it twice more across a paragraph. Your claims don't usually need the runway. If the piece circles back to the same point more than twice, cut the repetitions.
17. **Lazy extremes and empty adverbs** — "every," "always," "never," "everyone"; "really," "just," "genuinely," "actually," "literally." Extremes claim nothing specific; adverbs emphasise nothing. Replace with the specific, or cut.

## Quotation and typography

When revising, also enforce these source conventions:

- **Attribution**: every quotation must have `"quoted text" — Surname` format (curly doubles, em-dash, surname). Apply even when the poet is named in the surrounding sentence — the attribution is a typographic flag for the rendering pipeline.
- **Verse line breaks**: use ` / ` (space-slash-space) within inline quotations. Don't flatten verse into prose.
- **Titles**: long works in markdown italic (`*In Memoriam*`), short works in single curly quotes (`'Sailing to Byzantium'`).
- **Ellipsis**: bracketed `[…]` for omissions in quoted material. Unbracketed `…` only in the writer's own prose.
- **Nested quotation**: single curly quotes inside double: `"as Keats said, 'beauty is truth'" — Surname`.
- **Opening**: no process narration. A cold reader hasn't seen the stimulus or the retrieval. Start at the first real claim.
